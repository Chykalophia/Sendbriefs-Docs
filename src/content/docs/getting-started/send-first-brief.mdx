## The mental model

A brief in SendBriefs is built around three ideas:

1. **A client and a period.** Briefs belong to a specific client over a specific reporting period (last month, last week, etc.). The "Add to a brief" flow resolves which draft brief is the right one to contribute to.
2. **Four fixed sections.** Every brief is organized into four content buckets: ✅ **Done**, 🔄 **In Progress**, 📝 **To Do**, 📌 **Additional Notes**. These are organizational, not workflow stages — you fill in any combination that applies to this period.
3. **Blocks inside sections.** Each section is a stack of blocks (text, headings, metrics, tables, callouts, images, charts). The editor's `/` slash-menu adds blocks.

Each brief moves through a state machine: `draft → pending_approval → approved → sent`, with `changes_requested` as a side path.

## Step 1: Click "Add to a brief"

In the left sidebar, under **REPORTS**, click **Add to a brief**. You'll land at `/reports/update`.

<Placeholder type="screenshot" caption="The Add to a brief landing page — client picker on the left, recent clients listed" />

## Step 2: Pick the client

If you arrived from a client page, the client is pre-selected. Otherwise, pick from the client list.

Once a client is selected, SendBriefs **resolves the contribution target**:

- **If there's an open draft brief for this client's current period**, you land directly in the composer with an "Adding to: [brief title]" line.
- **If there's no draft yet** and the client has a configured template + cadence, SendBriefs creates a new draft brief for the period and drops you into it.
- **If the period brief has already been sent or approved** with no open draft, you'll see a **disambiguation screen** offering to reopen the sent brief, start the next period's brief, or cancel.
- **If the client isn't configured for the Living period flow** (no template / contributions disabled / non-periodic client), you'll see the **deliberate authoring chooser** — start a one-off brief, or pick a template.

For your first time, the third bullet's "start the next period's brief" is the common path if you haven't built one for this client before.

## Step 3: Fill in the four sections

The editor shows the four sections stacked, each collapsible. Click any section header to expand.

<Placeholder type="screenshot" caption="Brief editor — four sections collapsed at the top, one expanded showing blocks inside" />

For each section, type your update or use **`/`** to open the block picker. Available block types include:

- **Paragraph, Heading, Quote, List, Checklist** — text and structure
- **Metric, Gauge, Sparkline, StatsGrid** — numeric callouts
- **Table** — tabular data
- **Image** — embedded screenshots or assets
- **Callout** — for wins, blockers, asks
- **Divider, Section, Column** — layout helpers
- **Sentiment, ChannelGrid** — specialized blocks

Empty sections render nothing in the final brief — only the sections you fill in show up to the recipient.

> **Workspace setting: layout mode.** Workspaces have a `strict` or `flexible` mode. In `strict`, every block must live inside one of the four sections. In `flexible`, top-level blocks alongside sections are allowed. Set per workspace, with per-template overrides — usually the default works.

## Step 4: Preview

Click **Preview** at the top of the editor. The brief renders the way the client will see it in their portal — branded chrome, your colors, your logo.

<Placeholder type="screenshot" caption="Preview pane showing the rendered brief with the client's branding" />

What to check:

- **Are the right sections populated?** Empty sections are hidden in the rendered output.
- **Do the numbers and dates make sense?** Most data in the current preview is written directly; data integrations are limited (see [Integrations](/features/integrations/)).
- **Does the branding match expectations?** Logo and accent colors.
- **Does the period header read correctly?**

## Step 5: Submit for approval (if your workspace requires it)

If your workspace requires approvals, click **Submit for approval**. The brief moves to `pending_approval` and assigned approvers get a `report_submitted` notification.

Approvers can:

- **Approve** — brief moves to `approved`. From here, you (or the approver) can send it.
- **Request changes** — brief moves to `changes_requested` with a note for the author. You fix and resubmit.

If approvals aren't required in your workspace, you'll see the **Send** option directly.

## Step 6: Send a test to yourself first

Before the client sees anything, click **Send test to me** (or the equivalent menu option). The brief delivers to *your* email so you can:

- See it in your actual inbox (rendering can differ from preview).
- Click through any links to make sure they work, including the portal link.
- Confirm subject line and "from" name look right.

This catches problems the preview pane can't surface — typically email client rendering differences.

## Step 7: Send to the client

When the test looks right:

1. Click **Send to client**.
2. Confirm the recipient list (every contact on this client with `receive reports` on).
3. Confirm.

The brief moves to `sent`. You'll see a confirmation, and the brief appears in the **Sent** tab on the Reports list.

> For recurring monthly briefs, scheduling lives at **Settings → Reports → Scheduling**, not on the individual brief. See [Monthly client reporting](/flows/monthly-reporting/) for the full schedule-and-forget workflow.

## Step 8: Watch delivery

Open the sent brief and look at the engagement bar at the top. It shows, per recipient:

- **Delivered** — SendGrid accepted and forwarded the email (usually within 30 seconds).
- **Opened** — recipient loaded the tracking pixel (best-effort — privacy filters mask this).
- **Clicked** — any tracked link was clicked.
- **Portal views** — recipient opened the brief in the hosted portal. Often the cleanest engagement signal.

Don't read too much into "0 opens" — many email clients block tracking. Portal views are usually a better signal.

## You did it

You shipped a brief. Every brief after this one is faster — the four sections become muscle memory, and your saved templates speed up the structure for new clients.
