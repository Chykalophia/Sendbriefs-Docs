Annual reviews aren't bigger monthly briefs — they're a different shape. Build them in December (or your fiscal Q4) using twelve months of data. Plan <strong>30-45 minutes per client</strong> of human-written commentary on top of the auto-rendered numbers. Send the first week of January.

## Why annual reviews are different

A monthly brief is news — "here's what happened in May." An annual review is narrative — "here's the year, here's what we did about it, here's where we go next."

The data work is automated. The narrative work isn't.

## Suggested structure

A reasonable annual-review brief has these sections, in order:

1. **Year headline** — the one number that defines the year (revenue, growth rate, total leads — whichever is the centerpiece).
2. **Year-over-year comparison** — this year vs. last, broken out by quarter.
3. **Quarterly trend chart** — the headline metric across the last 8 quarters if you have it.
4. **Highlight reel** — top campaigns, biggest wins, key moments. Prose plus 3-4 mini-stat callouts.
5. **What didn't work + lessons** — honest, specific. This block is what makes the brief feel real.
6. **Goals vs. actuals** — if you set targets at the start of the year, how they landed.
7. **Next year's focus** — three priorities for the coming year. Prose, written by you.
8. **Account team note** — a personal signoff from the lead.
9. **Renewal CTA** *(optional)* — a soft prompt about next year's engagement.

You can swap blocks. The structure matters more than the specific items — annual reviews tell a story, and stories have shape.

## Building it: a Q4 workflow

Block out **half a day** in early-to-mid December. The commentary work is the time sink, not the data.

### Build the template once

Save a strong annual-review brief as a template — "Annual review — house style." Set its cadence to `milestone` or `custom` since this isn't a regular monthly cadence.

You'll reuse this template every year and across every client. Per-client variation lives in the prose blocks and in the data bindings.

### For each client

1. Click **Add to a brief** in the sidebar.
2. Pick the client.
3. SendBriefs resolves the contribution target. For an annual review, the client likely doesn't have an open "current period" draft for the year — you'll land in the deliberate authoring chooser. Pick **from template** and choose your "Annual review — house style" template.
4. The brief is created for the period (typically Jan 1 to Dec 31 in the client's time zone, or your fiscal year).
5. **Fill in the sections.** If you're using the starter template, that's the same four — Done / In Progress / To Do / Additional Notes — repurposed for annual content:
   - **✅ Done** — the year's highlight reel, 3-4 concrete moments.
   - **🔄 In Progress** — what's mid-flight that carries into next year.
   - **📝 To Do** — next year's three priorities.
   - **📌 Additional Notes** — what didn't work + lessons; the account team note.

   Or — if you've built a dedicated annual-review template — the sections you defined for it. The shape matters more than the names.

6. **Sanity-check the render.** There's no "send test to me" button on a brief; use the Preview pane, the Email Layout's "Email myself" button, or send to a smoke-test client first.

Repeat for every client. If you have 20 clients, this is roughly 10-15 hours of writing. It's also the highest-leverage 15 hours of the year — these briefs end up in renewal conversations more often than any monthly does.

### Schedule

Set the brief to send on the first business day of January (or fiscal Q1). Strongly recommended: turn on internal approval so a teammate reads each one before send. Annual reviews are the worst place to ship a typo.

## The "what didn't work" block

This is the block that distinguishes a real review from a marketing exercise.

Don't write:

> 2025 was a successful year with growth across all channels.

Write:

> Organic traffic dropped 18% after the May Google update. We responded with a content audit in June and recovered ~12% of the loss by Q4. The remaining gap is structural — we'll address it in 2026 with [specific plan].

Be specific about what underperformed and what you'd do differently. Clients trust agencies that own mistakes more than agencies that perform competence.

## The "next year's focus" block

Three priorities is the right number. Two feels thin. Four loses focus.

Each priority should have:

- A one-sentence statement.
- One paragraph of context.
- A specific deliverable or milestone.

Example:

> **Priority 2: rebuild the abandoned-cart flow.**
>
> Current recovery is 8% — industry benchmark for your category is 12-16%. We've identified specific friction points in the current flow and have a redesign queued.
>
> **Milestone:** new flow live by end of Q1 2026. Target: 12% recovery by end of Q2.

This block doubles as your engagement roadmap. Reference it in the year's monthly briefs.

## Renewal CTA: soft, not pushy

If you include a renewal block, keep it light:

> Our retainer renews on [date]. We'd love to keep building together. Reply or grab a slot on [calendar link] to discuss the year ahead.

Don't make this the loudest block. The brief is the value demonstration — the renewal is the natural consequence.

## What to do if a client churns mid-year

Generate a **partial-year review** instead. Same template, but the period is "engagement start to last brief date."

This is a relationship-closer. Even when an engagement ends, sending a thoughtful summary of what was accomplished leaves a positive door open.

The renewal CTA doesn't apply here. Replace it with a "thanks for the trust" note.

## Working with historical data

Annual reviews reference 12 months of data. A few things to check:

- **Integration continuity.** If the GA4 connection broke or was reconnected mid-year, the year-over-year chart may have a gap or skew. Spot-check the data; add a note in prose if there's an anomaly to explain.
- **Period boundaries.** Calendar year in the client's time zone is the standard. If you operate on a fiscal year, set the period start/end manually to match.
- **Trend chart sample size.** If you've only worked with this client for 6 months, you only have 2 quarters of data. The chart will show what's available; just acknowledge it in prose ("our first half of the engagement").

## Schedule and approvals

For the actual send:

- Send the first business day of January (or fiscal Q1).
- Send between 9-10 am client-local (matches your monthly cadence).
- **Turn on internal approval** for annual reviews even if you don't use it for monthlies. A second reader catches typos and tone issues that you'll miss after staring at the brief for 45 minutes.

## Common gotchas

- **Don't ship the same prose to multiple clients.** It's tempting after writing 20 of these. But "Priority 2 for 2026" should be specific to each client. Clients catch generic copy faster than you'd think.
- **Time zones for "the year" boundary.** A calendar year in a client's Sydney time zone may differ from yours in Chicago by a day. Use the client's time zone — same convention as monthly briefs.
- **Goals-vs-actuals only works if goals were set.** If you didn't track explicit goals, either skip that block or write the goals retrospectively (and label them as retrospective).
- **Trend chart can look thin for newer clients.** If you have only 2-4 quarters of data, the chart shows what it can. Acknowledge in prose if needed.
